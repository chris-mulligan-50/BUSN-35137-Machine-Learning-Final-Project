Tree Structure; “A Flowchart”
Fully nonparametric and departs from the traditional regression approach we’ve seen up to this
point.
▶ “Grow” the tree in sequential steps, each step creating a new “branch” that sorts the data
leftover from the previous step into bins based on the predictors
▶ Sequentially branching slices predictor space into rectangular partitions
▶ Forecast outcome in partition with average value of the outcome variable in that partition

Growing a Tree

Boosted Trees
Boosting: ensemble method for aggregating many individually “weak learners” (models that don’t
forecast well) into a single “strong learner”.
Algorithm sketch
1. Fix tree depthL(generally shallow e.g.L=1)
2. Fit 1st simple tree
▶ Given limited depth will be a weak learner with high bias
3. Fit 2nd shallow tree toresiduals from the first tree
4. Forecasts from the two trees are aggregated to form ensemble prediction
▶ Shrink forecast from 2nd tree by a factor of𝜂∈ (0,1)
▶ Helps prevent the model from overfitting to residuals
5. Iterate until total ofBtrees in the ensemble
L,𝜂, andBare tuning parameters selected via cross-validation.

Random Forests
Bagging: ensemble method that bootstraps many datasets, fits models to each then averages their

▶ Individual trees are deep and overfit, thus inefficiently variable
▶ Variance reduction via average overBbootstrap samples – stabilizes predictive performance
Dropout: Variation on bagging to de-correlate bootstrap trees
▶ If, e.g., firm size is a dominant predictor, then most bagged trees will share low-level splits on
size, resulting in a high correlation among their predictions
▶ Consider only a (random) subset ofMpredictors to split each branch
▶ Enhances variance reduction compared to standard bagging
▶ Biggest expected improvement with strong predictors AND moderately important predictors
OR highly correlated predictors

Origins of Neural Networks
▶ Modeling the human brain:
▶ Human brain, through billions of interactions among hundreds of millions of neurons, learns to
solve (some) complex problems nearly instantly (like face recognition)
▶ Mathematical model of neuron function for decision making (McCulloch and Pitts, 1943)
▶ Early speculation about learning via neuron models
▶ Confluence of computer science and statistics:
▶ In conventional programming, tell computer what to do, breaking big problems into many small,
precisely defined tasks
▶ Statistical models are canvas upon which data paints coarse scientific pictures
▶ Learn (estimate) from observational data, instead of requiring pre-specified logic, for decision
making and problem solving

--- Page 108 ---

Why Neural Networks?
▶ Theoretical underpinnings as "universal approximators" for any smooth predictive association
(e.g., Hornik, 1991)
▶ Though many other nonparametric methods can do the same.
▶ Flexibility draws from ability to intertwine many telescoping layers of nonlinear predictor
interactions (hence synonym "deep learning")
▶ At the same time, their complexity ranks them least transparent, least interpretable, and most
highly parameterized ML tools
